# Hand & Wrist Acupoints (穴位) — Research Link Library

Compiled for AcuGuide Hand Coach · 2026-06-20

**How to read this:** Sources are grouped by purpose and tagged for authority.
🟢 = high-authority / peer-reviewed / official body · 🟡 = practitioner/educational reference (useful for locations, weak for efficacy claims) · 🔴 = critical / skeptical appraisal. For a wellness product, lean on 🟢 for any efficacy language, use 🟡 only for *where the point is*, and read 🔴 so your claims survive a skeptical judge.

## 1. Authoritative standards for point *locations* (the "where")

These define where points actually are — the safest basis for your overlay geometry and copy.

- 🟢 **WHO Standard Acupuncture Point Locations in the Western Pacific Region (2008)** — the official international consensus standardizing 361 point locations. The canonical authority for *location*. Official record: https://iris.who.int/items/f188654a-d8a7-4519-9979-8e2de713c060 · PubMed listing: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/19204011/ · Overview: https://books.google.com/books/about/WHO_Standard_Acupuncture_Point_Locations.html?id=_Wwys_i2Ks8C
- 🟡 **Atlas of Acupuncture Points (point-location compilation, chiro.org PDF)** — printable location atlas: https://chiro.org/acupuncture/ABSTRACTS/Acupuncture_Points.pdf

## 3. Effectiveness evidence (the "does it work")

### PC6 for nausea/vomiting — the strongest hand-point evidence
- 🟢 **Cochrane review** of P6 acupoint stimulation for postoperative nausea & vomiting (PONV): 26 trials, >3,000 patients; real P6 superior to sham. PubMed summary: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16813514/ · ResearchGate: https://www.researchgate.net/publication/6970345
- 🟢 **PLOS ONE meta-analysis** — acupuncture for prevention/treatment of PONV: https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0082474 (PMC: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3862842/)
- 🟢 **Network meta-analysis (2025)** — PC6 stimulation for preventing PONV: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/40938065/
- 🟡 Note from the evidence: *acupressure* (vs needling) helped first-day nausea but not vomiting; wrist-band electrical devices were not effective — method matters.

### LI4 (Hegu) for pain / dysmenorrhea / labor
- 🟢 RCT — noninvasive electroacupuncture at LI4 + SP6 for dysmenorrhea: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22339102/
- 🟢 RCT — Hegu acupressure for cannulation pain: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/41570661/ (ScienceDirect: https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S155083072600011X)
- 🟡 RCT — LI4 acupressure on labor pain: https://www.midwifery.iocspublisher.org/index.php/midwifery/article/view/410

### Acupressure for menstrual pain (dysmenorrhea)
- 🟢 Systematic review of RCTs of acupressure for primary dysmenorrhea: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/24023570/
- 🟢 SP6 acupuncture/acupressure for dysmenorrhea — meta-analysis: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3600281/
- 🟢 TCM meridian-theory interventions for dysmenorrhea — network meta-analysis (Frontiers, 2024): https://www.frontiersin.org/journals/medicine/articles/10.3389/fmed.2024.1453609/full
- ⚠️ Scope note: SP6 (the best-evidenced menstrual point) is on the **leg**, outside a hand-only MVP.

### Acupuncture/acupressure for tension & migraine headache
- 🟢 Systematic review & network meta-analysis, tension-type headache (2023): https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC10325685/
- 🟢 Overview of systematic reviews, acupuncture for TTH (notes review quality is "critically low"): https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC7106880/
- 🟢 Manual therapy & acupuncture for TTH — systematic review: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8483450/

## 4. Official / authority assessments of acupuncture overall

- 🟢 **NCCIH (NIH) — Acupuncture: Effectiveness and Safety**: the most balanced government summary. Moderate-quality evidence for migraine frequency reduction and chronic low-back pain; for osteoarthritis the gap vs *sham* is small. https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/acupuncture-effectiveness-and-safety
- 🟢 **NCCIH — Complementary Health Approaches for Chronic Pain (provider digest)**: https://www.nccih.nih.gov/health/providers/digest/complementary-health-approaches-for-chronic-pain-science
- 🟢 **StatPearls (NCBI) — Acupuncture**: clinical reference overview: https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK532287/
- 🟡 **WHO — Acupuncture: Review and Analysis of Reports on Controlled Clinical Trials (2002/2003)**: often cited as listing ~29 conditions "proven" effective, but it is **widely criticized** as methodologically weak and not a modern evidence standard — cite with caution. Reference: https://www.sciepub.com/reference/76234
- 🟢 **NIH Consensus Statement on Acupuncture (1997)**: historical baseline: https://www.encyclopedia.com/science/medical-magazines/consensus-statement-acupuncture

## 5. Validity, criticism & the "do acupoints exist" debate

Read these so your product claims stay defensible. The honest scientific picture: evidence is strongest for **nausea (PC6)** and **some chronic pain/headache**, but much of it is low-quality, and high-quality trials often show **little difference between real and sham point location**.

**Skeptical / critical appraisals**
- 🔴 **Edzard Ernst — "Acupuncture: a critical analysis"** (J Internal Medicine, 2006); review of 57 systematic reviews found "little truly convincing evidence" for pain relief: https://onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1111/j.1365-2796.2005.01584.x · PubMed: https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/16420542/
- 🔴 Ernst — "Acupuncture: what does the most reliable evidence tell us?" (J Pain Symptom Manage): https://www.jpsmjournal.com/article/S0885-3924(08)00452-1/fulltext
- 🔴 Ernst blog — "no convincing evidence it is an effective therapy": https://edzardernst.com/2018/01/acupuncture-there-is-no-convincing-evidence-that-it-is-an-effective-therapy/
- 🔴 **Science-Based Medicine — Acupuncture Revisited**: argues placement doesn't matter, effects are placebo-consistent: https://sciencebasedmedicine.org/acupuncture-revisited/
- 🔴 Ernst — "Acupuncture: a treatment to die for?" (safety/adverse events): https://journals.sagepub.com/doi/10.1258/jrsm.2010.100181

**Methodology problem — sham controls**
- 🟢 "Sham Acupressure Controls Used in RCTs: A Systematic Review and Critique" (PLOS ONE) — why acupressure trials are hard to blind and interpret: https://journals.plos.org/plosone/article?id=10.1371/journal.pone.0132989

**Pro-validity / anatomical-basis research (counterpoint)**
- 🟢 Langevin & Yandow — "Relationship of acupuncture points and meridians to connective tissue planes" (The Anatomical Record, 2002); ~80% correspondence with connective-tissue planes: https://anatomypubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/10.1002/ar.10185
- 🟡 "Review of evidence that the fascia network could be the anatomical basis for acupoints/meridians" (PMC): https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3092510/

## 6. Bottom line for AcuGuide's claims

The defensible position, supported by the 🟢 sources above:

1. **PC6 for nausea/queasiness** has the best evidence of any hand/wrist point (Cochrane-level) — but the effect is clearest for needling/acupressure on *postoperative* nausea, and acupressure helps nausea more than vomiting.
2. **Headache and menstrual pain** have *some* supportive reviews, but quality is low and real-vs-sham differences are often small.
3. **Where you press may matter less than the trials assume** — multiple high-quality studies find sham points perform similarly. This is exactly why AcuGuide should sell **guided, safe self-care form** ("press here, hold steady, breathe, stop on red flags") rather than **clinical efficacy**. That framing is both more honest and more defensible.
